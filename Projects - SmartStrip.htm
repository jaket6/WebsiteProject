SmartStrip
May 21, 2014



The idea for this Senior Design Project came about on a lazy summer day. After waking up at about 10 in the morning, I moved into the living room to continue loafing about. My goal was simple enough, find the television remote so I could tune into some daytime ESPN programming. The only problem was the misplaced clicker which escaped my search. At that point, I decided to forgo watching TV all together and made the transition to my tablet’s internet connection.

That’s when it hit me. I can’t even be bothered to turn on the television without a remote, so why should I be bothered to turn on my lamp, my computer, or my coffee maker without one either. Why hasn’t someone invented a way to control all of these electronics without having to physically get up? Is it even possible? Can I make this? I need a Senior Project after all…

With this idea in mind, I teamed up with my trusted EE 296 and EE 396 partner and we began to come up with the initial designs for this project. Many decisions such as form-factor, functionality, hardware, and software components still had to be sorted out.

So what are we even making?

After many meetings, the SmartStrip concept came into focus. Our goal was to make “smart” power strip which could control the On/Off status of its respective outlets. Other features such as automatic timers, power limit settings, power consumption data, and monetary visualizations were added in during brainstorming process. All of these features would need to be displayed in some way to the users, thus, an Android application became necessary to the functionality of the project.

Hardware


The hardware components were the most tangible parts of the project. We needed to find elements which could accomplish all of our objectives at a relatively low cost. First, we needed way to communicate between the power strip and the smart phone/tablet.

Wifi? Xbee? Bluetooth? Which one of these protocol presented the best option? Well Wifi modules are relatively expensive and consume large amount of power while transmitting data. These modules would enable access to the internet at the cost of added design complexity. A web server which could store data and respond to client request would need to be implemented to establish communication between the two devices. These obstacles eliminated Wifi from our choices of communication protocol.

What about Xbee? This method does allow point to point communication and is relatively inexpensive. No server setup is required and a variety of online resources are available for reference. But, your average tablet or smart phone does not communicate using Xbee protocol. An attachment/plug-in hardware component would have to be physically connected to the device in order to establish communication. The lack of native hardware support made the Xbee undesirable.

The only remaining option was Bluetooth. The cheap $8 modules made it an unbeatable value while its universally supported hardware made it accessible from any smart phone or tablet. The availability of online resources along with its compatibility with the Arduino microcontroller made it the most logical choice for our application.

Next, the ability to control the status of each outlet had to be addressed. The only viable option available to us were relays. Dealing with 120 Volts and potentially high currents eliminated the smaller, low-power control circuits. Relays (controlled mechanical switches) gave us the flexibility to fully control the On/Off status of each outlet.

Finally, we needed a way to measure the power consumption of each outlet. Luckily, the fixed wall voltage of 120 Volts meant that we only needed current metering capabilities to calculate the instantaneous power of the device. Again, large voltages (and potentially high currents) eliminated the option for inline current metering circuits. This left the non-invasive option. Current sensing transformers (which output a proportional voltage) allowed us to measure the current flowing through each outlet without affecting the circuit's operation.

Additional components such as wires, terminals, amplifier boards, and inlet switches were used to complete the design. Most were only necessary for the prototype version of the product.


Software


The Android development environment was the best option for developing our first application. This app in particular had to display large amounts of data while allowing the user to operate all of the features we aimed to create.

Eventually, a design/layout of the application's display was settled on. The main On/Off buttons were located up top with the power consumption data being displayed directly below. Power Limit settings were programmed and enabled users to turn the outlets off after the desired amount of power had been consumed (i.e. 1 KWh). The bottom portion of the app held the timer setting. This allowed the users to specify when the outlet would turn On/Off without having to actively change the status.

Finally, A Money Tracker Tab was introduced to better visualize the cost of each outlet. Live graphing features displayed the instantaneous power consumption while accumulated costs for each outlet were displayed below.


Fabrication


After many hours of troubleshooting, the hardware was functional. It was now possible to achieve Bluetooth communication between the two devices and control the status of each outlet.

Now, it was time to retrieve power consumption information from the CT Transformers. Our group ran into an unforeseen obstacle after testing the output of the device. The CT Transformer was limited to an induced current of 30 Amps. This value was scaled and ran through a burden resistor which output a voltage value between 0 and 1 Volt. Unfortunately, the Arduino’s 10-bit ADC had an input range of 0 to 5 Volts meaning much of the ADC's resolution was not being utilized. The solution came in the form an amplifier board which took the input of the CT transformer (35mm headphone jack) and applied a gain of 4 to the output. Two low-power quad-OpAmps were used to achieve this gain and improve the resolution of the device. The result was a more accurate reading of each outlet's instantaneous power consumption.



This left us with a working hardware device. Bluetooth communication, power consumption values, On/Off control and power limit/timer features now operated correctly. It was now time to put everything in a nice, clean enclosure. The original plan was to 3D-print a custom fitting case which would complete the design, however, all of the on-campus 3D printers were out of commission during the final weeks of the semester making a custom printed case impossible.

Our only option was to construct an enclosure ourselves.


This makeshift design left something to be desired. Future cases would allow for a smaller form-factor and more professional appearance.

Maybe a coat of paint will help?


The bottom of the case was left transparent so the internal circuitry could be viewed.


Results


With the assembly completed, our final design accomplished all of the goals which were set at beginning of the project.


This demo quickly displays the On/Off capability of each outlet, power consumption display, power limit settings, timer control, and cost visualization of the application.

Our group was able to fully complete the projects intended goals which utilized skills and knowledge obtained from the College of Engineering's curriculum. SmartStrip was also featured in the 2014 PACE Business Plan Competition where the group advanced into the Semi-final round.
