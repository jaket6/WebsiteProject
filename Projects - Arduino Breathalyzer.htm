Arduino Breathalyzer
November 8, 2014

How much is too much? It's a common question we ask ourselves, especially in the college environment. When it comes to alcohol, we tend to disregard the voice of reason in our heads in favor of creating a memorable (sometimes impossible-to-remember) experience. You'd think that a bunch of 18-22 year-olds would be the worst offenders, and you would be correct. But, second place should be awarded to the middle-aged working man. The corporate representative who's been forced to attend a college banquet because the higher-ups can't be bothered to go. The only redeeming quality of this Tuesday night held hostage is the promise of a little entertainment (via student organization booths), a free meal, and of course, and open bar.

This is where the idea of the Arduino Breathalyzer project originated. The 2013 Holmescoming Banquet.

Every student organization was expected to host a booth which displayed the skills and knowledge acquired from the College of Engineering's curriculum. The “best” booth would be awarded an extra $400 to spend at their discretion. Being a member of the IEEE student branch, it was my job to create a project which utilized the skills we have gained through EE while also providing entertainment for our generous guests.

Knowing that an open bar was the main draw of the event, settling on a visual breathalyzer display came about naturally and, with the help of some other IEEE members, we were able to create a winning project.

Design


After designing a rough concept for this project, it was important to find hardware which was capable of meeting all of our requirements. We wanted to measure the alcohol levels of the guests and display it in visually pleasing fashion. Christmas lights were chosen as our medium (since flashing lights scream EE). Now, finding a sensor that could detect the user's alcohol levels became a priority. Luckily, a breakout board for a similar project was available online which utilized an affordable MQ-3 Alcohol Gas Sensor. The DrinkShield Board was only compatible with an Arduino Uno which effectively ended our search for a microcontroller.


Now that we could sense alcohol levels, we needed to consider our options regarding the array of Christmas lights. The Arduino's output pins are limited to 5 Volts DC at 40 milliAmps. This is nowhere near the 120Volts AC the Christmas lights ran off of. We would require “wall outlet” levels of power to operate the array of lights.


To control the switching of these lights, power to the outlets would need to be variable. Two states of On and Off would suffice. These specifications led us to select relays as our source of power control. The mechanical relay switches we selected were compatible with a variety of microcontrollers. It operated on digital logic of 5 Volts, matching the Arduino's output range while requiring only 5 Volts of power to activate the internal switches. This compatibility between the two systems made it easy to integrate into the working project.


Next, a wiring scheme had to be designed to make distribute power across the 8-channel relays. A LOW input signal would activate the internal mechanical switch, completing the circuit and allowing power to flow to the outlet receptacle.


Everything was assembled and the connections were double checked. The “hot” line was connected to the red wires which were in series with the relays. The green wires exiting the relays were connected to the outlet receptacles. The power would then flow through the plugged-in Christmas lights and the circuit would complete through ground (black). The relays, at any time, could be switched On or Off from the microcontroller. This state would then be mirrored by the string of lights, giving us the power to make them flash/flicker on command.


Now, All that was left was a quick coat of paint and the addition of the IEEE logo.


The availible software libraries were open-source and came from the DrinkShield website (linked below) which took care of the ADC conversion from the MQ-3 sensor. Some changes were made to the Arduino's outputs which varied the On/Off state of the relays proportional to alcohol levels. This created a flashing array of Christmas lights which entertained our guests and landed us in first place among the other booths.

Results



In this video, the Christmas lights are strung up in the background around a pair of palm trees. A demo is performed by blowing an equivalent of pure Tequila directly onto the MQ-3 sensor. This registered a maximum score which activated all 8 strings of lights.


I would recommend this project for anyone comfortable with developing on an Arduino. Wiring all of the outlet receptacles turned out to be the most challenging aspect if the project.
