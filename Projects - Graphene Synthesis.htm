Graphene Synthesis
May 14, 2013

Technology has been advancing at a steady and unyielding pace. What was once considered groundbreaking and revolutionary a few months ago will be considered mundane in the blink of an eye. The architecture, fabrication methods, and even materials which makeup the newest gadgets have been constantly evolving over time. The recorded shrinkage of the transistor (the foundation of modern-day electronics) has allowed our devices to become faster and more powerful, while also assuming a smaller form factor. Currently, silicon is the prevailing material from which we create these semiconductors, yet, we are fast approaching the physical limitations that this material can offer. 

To keep advancing technology at its current pace, a new material must seize silicon's semiconductor crown. The new wonder material in question hails from a more modest background. Pencil-lead. But, not just any type of lead (which is actually graphite) will do. A two-dimensional plane of carbon-graphite promises to aid all of these technological woes.



This nex-gen material was discovered by a research group at Manchester University in 2004. The first two-dimensional planes were created by placing small pieces of pencil graphite onto a roll of household Scotch Tape. The tape was pulled apart repeatedly until only a two-dimensional layer remained. Andre Giem and Konstantin Novselov were then awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2010 for their contributions to this research.

But what makes two-dimensional graphite (also know as graphene) so special? Well, its peculiar shape gives it attractive electrical properties. The planar material has a zero bandgap and high electron mobility making it the perfect candidate for electrical conduction. Research utilizing graphene as a material for super-capacitors would allow batteries to charge in a matter of seconds. Physical applications using graphene as a replacement for charcoal water filters has also received some attention. These promising characteristics make graphene out to be the perfect material. So what's the drawback?

It's expensive.

The best method of “mass producing” graphene required and expensive CVD (chemical vapor deposition) machine. These pieces of equipment can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars which puts large quantities out of reach of many research group. The goal of my junior project was to explore more cost effective ways of synthesizing this wonder-material to make it more accessible for research and development.

Design


This was not a standard design and fabrication project like most x96 classes. Rather, its focus centered on research and experimental design. The inspiration for our synthesis technique came from a UCLA research group who first performed this particular method in 2012. Our goal was to recreate the results of this experiment and attempt to improve its production yield.

Instead of chemically depositing layers of graphene through a CVD machine, the research group used a simple LightScribe disc drive. This drive was capable of etching images onto the top side of a blank CD using a lower power laser. After coating the top side of the CD with graphite-oxide, the laser “burned” the chemical into two-dimensional graphene.


After ordering 50mg of graphite-oxide, we tested multiple types of plastic which would place on the CD. Plastic folder sheets, transparency papers, and thin packaging plastics were coated with the liquid graphite-oxide and set to cure overnight.

When the liquid had solidified into a layers of hardened graphite-oxide, we programmed the LightScribe software to completely overlap the surface of the CD to avoid any gaps in coverage.


Results



When the burn cycle had finished, we inspected the layer of graphite-oxide to see if there was any distinguishable differences. We noted visible marks were the laser had made its passes and say a darkening of the chemically affected area.


When testing the conductive properties of the sample, we were unable to obtain an ohmic reading from the digital multimeter. Though this method is known to produce relatively low amounts of two-dimensional graphene, the proposed methods allows production on a very large scale. Improvements in equipment such as a higher definition LightScribe drive along with a graphite-oxide solution of higher concentration would better our results.


We were unable to determine if the laser passes were close enough to physically link the line of “burnt” graphite-oxide. This LightScribe drive was built-in to my current laptop which did not have the same power output or resolution of new models.


Though we were unable to successfully recreate the UCLA experimental method, out group was able to research a new and developing material along with designing experimental methods to produce it.
