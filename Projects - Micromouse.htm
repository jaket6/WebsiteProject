Micromouse
May 9, 2012



Cars, stealth drones, and vacuum cleaners. These are a few examples of autonomous robots making their way into our everyday lives. Micromouse, however, is an international competition which pits competing “mice” in a race against the clock to autonomously navigate to the center of a 16x16 maze. These tiny robots (limited to 16x16x16 cm) must be preprogrammed to intake sensor readings and avoid collision with the walls or corners of the maze.

This competition served as an introduction to group design projects and served as my 296 class. Basics skills such as circuit design, prototyping, fabrication, programming, and time management are all necessary to reach the center.



Design


Beginner teams must decide on a preliminary design for their mouse. A “top-down” sensing mouse uses multiple IR pairs which overhang the walls of the maze. When the sensors detect the walls on either side, the mouse is able to recognize its relative position within the cells to track properly. A “side-sensing” mouse utilizes IR sensors placed on the chassis which outputs analog values related to mouse’s the distance from the walls.

Our group decided to pursue the side-sensing design which allows for a smaller form factor. This design required slightly more complex software development as it made us of the microcontroller’s ADC.


A starting microcontroller along with a pair of stepper motors were provided as part of the 296 tutorials. The PIC18f4620 served as the “brain” of this project which used the MPLAB x v1.0 development environment. Four-volt unipolar stepper motors provided the power for this autonomous vehicle while Sharp GP2D120 IR sensors provided environmental feedback for the mouse.



The chassis which housed all of these components are shown below. Acrylic plexi-glass was used as the top and bottom layers of the mouse. A third contact point was fashioned with a plastic furniture slider while aluminum L-brackets held the motors and acrylic together.


Results



The competition allowed all of the x96 teams to compete and see where they rank among their peers. Throughout the development cycle, our team ran into many hardware problems. Some involved faulty sensors and motors while others resulted from poor soldering connections. As a result, the software team had little time to refine the wall-hugging algorithm which resulted in a poor performance during the competition

The group dynamics and time management aspects of this project proved to be valuable in future endeavors. Though the performance was undesirable, a lot was learned from participating in this project.
